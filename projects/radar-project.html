Case Study: Displacement Map for Taal Volcano (Philippines) — 12 January 2020

I selected Taal Volcano (large caldera in Taal Lake). I downloaded SAR images for November (before) and January (after) and performed the following steps:

Workflow

  1. Co-registration: Selected IW2 and VV polarization, performed orbit correction and stacked before/after sub-swaths to align pixels.
  2. Enhanced Spectral Diversity: Applied to improve coregistration (range/azimuth shift corrections and ionospheric correction).
  3. Interferogram formation: Cross-multiply master with complex conjugate of slave image to produce interferogram. Explained contributors to phase (flat-earth, topography, atmosphere, noise, displacement).
  4. Deburst: Remove seamlines between bursts to create continuous frames.
  5. Topographic removal: Remove topography to isolate displacement + atmosphere + noise. Interferogram shown in rainbow color scale (-π to +π).
  6. Multi-looking: Square pixels, trade off resolution for noise reduction (coarser resolution acceptable).
  7. Phase filtering: Filter noise (I used filter parameter ~0.6; adjust as needed).
  8. Phase unwrapping: SNAPHU import/export, unwrap the phase to produce continuous displacement map.
  9. Displacement map: Produce displacement output, apply terrain correction (add topography back) and export as GeoTIFF or for Google Earth.

Discussion

The fringes in the interferogram indicate ground deformation — the closer the fringes, the larger the displacement. For the Taal 2020 eruption the purple/pink colors indicate subsidence while blues indicate relative elevation change or uplift depending on color mapping. Terrain correction maps the displacement onto the real surface for interpretation.

Steps & Visuals

Key steps included co-registration, deburst, enhanced spectral diversity, interferogram formation, phase filtering, unwrapping and terrain correction. Each step improves the signal-to-noise ratio and isolates true displacement.
